Year in Review

llm-d: Thirteen Months That Reshaped Distributed LLM Inference

Markell Rawls | June 2026 | 18 min read
~4,500 words

May 2025. Red Hat Summit in Boston. The conference halls were filled with the usual buzz about Kubernetes operators, hybrid cloud strategies, and the latest OpenShift features. But this year, there was a different kind of energy in the air. The industry had been wrestling with a model-serving problem that was growing more acute by the month, and everyone in the room could feel it. vLLM had solved the single-node inference problem beautifully — PagedAttention was a genuine breakthrough, and serving a single large language model on a single GPU node was now efficient and well-understood. But that was never the real problem.

The real problem was what happened next. Organizations didn't need to serve one model. They needed to serve dozens of models across hundreds of GPUs, with predictable latency, controllable cost, and the kind of operational reliability that enterprise SLAs demand. The gap between "it works on one box" and "it works in production at scale" was enormous — and it was a gap that no existing project was seriously addressing. Load balancers didn't understand KV caches. Kubernetes autoscalers didn't understand the difference between prefill and decode. Inference frameworks were optimized for throughput on a single node, not for fleet-level efficiency across a heterogeneous GPU cluster.

llm-d was born to close that gap. Announced on that Summit stage by a coalition of companies that don't usually agree on anything, it proposed something audacious: a Kubernetes-native distributed inference framework that would make multi-model, multi-node, multi-accelerator LLM serving as natural as deploying a stateless web application. Thirteen months later, it is a CNCF Sandbox project with seven releases, thirteen-plus partners, and enterprise general availability status through Red Hat AI. This is the story of how it happened — told through the technical challenges, the people who solved them, and the ecosystem that formed around a shared vision for the future of AI inference infrastructure.

The Origin: Why llm-d Exists

To understand why llm-d needed to exist, you need to understand four technical insights that were well-known in the research community by early 2025 but had not yet been synthesized into a cohesive infrastructure project.

The KV Cache Routing Insight

Traditional load balancers — whether round-robin, least-connections, or even sophisticated weighted algorithms — treat all backend pods as interchangeable. For stateless web services, this is correct. For LLM inference, it is catastrophically wrong. Every LLM inference request builds a KV (key-value) cache as it processes the input prompt. This cache is the model's "working memory" for that conversation. When a request arrives at a pod that already has the relevant prefix cached from a previous request, the pod can skip the entire prefill computation — the most expensive phase of inference. Route that same request to a random pod, and you pay the full prefill cost again: every matrix multiplication, every attention computation, every layer of the transformer, re-executed from scratch.

The Core Routing Insight

A KV cache hit on the right pod can reduce time-to-first-token by 80% or more. This means the routing decision — which pod handles which request — is not a load-balancing problem. It is a cache topology problem. The router needs to know what every pod has cached and make placement decisions accordingly. This single insight became the foundation of the Endpoint Picker (EPP), llm-d's routing brain.

The Prefill/Decode Asymmetry

LLM inference has two distinct computational phases, and they have fundamentally different hardware requirements. Prefill is compute-bound: it processes the entire input prompt in parallel, performing massive matrix multiplications across every layer of the model. It wants raw FLOPS — the more tensor cores, the better. Decode is memory-bandwidth-bound: it generates one token at a time, and for each token, it reads the entire KV cache from GPU memory. It wants high memory bandwidth and large memory capacity, not necessarily peak compute.

Running both phases on the same GPU is a compromise. During prefill, the GPU's memory bandwidth sits largely idle. During decode, the GPU's compute units sit largely idle. Disaggregating these phases — routing prefill to compute-optimized pods and decode to memory-optimized pods — was a known optimization, but nobody had built the infrastructure to do it automatically at fleet scale with efficient KV cache transfer between the pods.

The Multi-Model Problem

Enterprises don't serve one model. They serve Llama for general conversation, a fine-tuned CodeLlama for code generation, a Mistral variant for summarization, several LoRA adapters for domain-specific tasks, and maybe a DeepSeek model for reasoning-heavy workloads. Each model has different hardware requirements, different scaling characteristics, and different latency targets. LoRA adapters add another dimension: the same base model might serve dozens of adapters, and routing needs to consider which adapters are loaded on which pods. The routing layer needs to understand all of this — model families, adapter states, hardware capabilities, and workload characteristics — to make intelligent placement decisions.

The Kubernetes-Native Imperative

The final insight was architectural: inference infrastructure should compose with existing cloud-native tooling, not require a parallel universe of custom infrastructure. Organizations had already invested heavily in Kubernetes, Gateway API, Prometheus, HPA, service mesh, and the entire CNCF ecosystem. An inference framework that required its own scheduling, its own monitoring, its own autoscaling, and its own networking was dead on arrival in enterprises that had standardized on Kubernetes. llm-d was designed from the start to be a Kubernetes-native citizen: Gateway API for ingress, Prometheus for metrics, HPA for scaling, standard container images, standard Helm charts, standard everything.

The Core Architecture

These four insights crystallized into a core architecture centered on the EPP (Endpoint Picker). The EPP is the routing brain of llm-d. It sits in the data path between the Gateway API and the inference pods, scoring each candidate pod based on multiple signals: KV cache match ratio (how much of the request's prefix is already cached), queue depth (how many requests are waiting), GPU utilization (how loaded the pod is), model and adapter state (which model and LoRA adapters are loaded), and health status (is the pod responsive and ready). The EPP makes a routing decision in microseconds, attaching a routing hint to the request that the Gateway API proxy uses to forward the traffic to the selected pod. It is lightweight, stateless in itself (it reads pod state from metrics endpoints), and designed to be the single point of intelligence in a system that is otherwise composed of standard Kubernetes primitives.

Upstream First, Enterprise Alongside

Most companies build internally first and open-source later, often years later, often with the most valuable pieces held back. Red Hat took a different approach with llm-d: they announced the Red Hat AI Inference Server — an enterprise productization built on vLLM + llm-d — simultaneously with the open-source project launch. This "upstream first, enterprise alongside" model meant that every feature would land in the open-source project before (or at the same time as) the enterprise product. It was a bet that open-source velocity would outpace proprietary development, and thirteen months later, that bet has paid off.

The first tagged release, v0.1.0, shipped within days of the Summit announcement. It was modest in scope — basic inference routing, the EPP foundation, Gateway API integration, and a health-check sidecar — but the architecture was sound. Performance was approximately 45 tokens per second on a baseline configuration, nowhere near production numbers. But the scaffolding was in place, and the release cadence that would define the project's trajectory had begun.

The Release Cadence: Deep Dive into Each Version

Seven releases in thirteen months. Each one built on the previous, each one addressing the next bottleneck in the journey from single-node prototype to fleet-scale production system. The trajectory tells a story of a project that knew where it was going and executed relentlessly.

v0.1 (May 2025) — Foundation

The first release was about proving the architecture. The core EPP scoring algorithm shipped in its initial form: a weighted combination of KV cache affinity, queue depth, and pod health. Gateway API integration established the pattern that would carry through every subsequent release — llm-d would be a Gateway API extension, not a replacement. The health-check sidecar provided the liveness and readiness signals that Kubernetes needed to manage inference pods correctly. Throughput sat at approximately 45 tokens per second on a single-pod deployment. The number was almost irrelevant; what mattered was that the control plane worked, the routing decisions were correct, and the architecture could scale. The "it compiles and routes correctly" release.

v0.2 "First Well-Lit Paths" (July 2025) — The Thesis Proven

v0.2 was the release that turned llm-d from an interesting architecture into a performance story. Three major features shipped simultaneously, and each one attacked a different bottleneck.

Prefix-cache-aware scheduling was the headline. The EPP now scored pods based on how much of the incoming request's prefix was already present in the pod's KV cache. A pod with 90% of the prefix cached would score dramatically higher than a pod with 0%. In practice, this meant that sequential requests from the same user, or requests that shared system prompts and few-shot examples, would consistently route to pods that could serve them with minimal prefill. Cache hit rates above 80% became routine for workloads with common prefixes.

Prefill/decode disaggregation landed in the same release. Separate pod pools for prefill (compute-optimized) and decode (memory-optimized), with KV cache transfer between them managed by a new pd-sidecar container. The handoff was transparent to the client — a request hit the EPP, got routed to a prefill pod, completed prefill, had its KV cache transferred to a decode pod, and began generating tokens, all without the client knowing that two different pods were involved.

MoE (Mixture of Experts) support arrived with DeepSeek-R1, including expert parallelism that distributed different experts across different GPUs. This was a signal that llm-d was thinking ahead — MoE models were becoming the dominant architecture for frontier models, and supporting them natively was essential.

The "Well-Lit Paths" naming was deliberate philosophy, not marketing. Each "path" was a tested, validated, opinionated deployment pattern: single-node inference, disaggregated prefill/decode, multi-node MoE. Users picked a path, not a pile of configuration knobs. Throughput jumped to approximately 200 tokens per second with prefix caching and disaggregation active.

v0.3 "Wider Well-Lit Paths" (October 2025) — Going Cross-Platform

v0.3 was the release that proved llm-d was not just an NVIDIA story. Three developments made this clear.

Inference Gateway v1.0 GA brought the gateway component to production readiness. This was the production-hardened ingress layer that sat in front of the EPP, handling TLS termination, rate limiting, and the Gateway API protocol translation that made llm-d composable with any Kubernetes environment.

Cross-platform hardware support expanded to Intel XPU (Gaudi accelerators) and Google TPU alongside NVIDIA GPUs. This was a significant engineering effort — the EPP's scoring algorithm, the health-check sidecar, and the disaggregation logic all needed to work correctly on hardware with very different memory architectures, compute characteristics, and driver stacks. But it was essential: enterprises choosing llm-d needed to know they were not locked into a single hardware vendor.

NIXL integration brought NVIDIA's Interconnect Library into the disaggregation story. NIXL enabled fast, low-latency KV cache transfer between prefill and decode pods using RDMA and NVLink where available. This was the piece that made disaggregation practical at scale — without fast cache transfer, the latency penalty of moving KV state between pods would have negated the throughput benefit of running each phase on optimized hardware.

Wide-EP (Expert Parallelism across nodes) enabled expert parallelism to span multiple nodes, not just multiple GPUs within a node. On H200 clusters, this delivered 2,200 tokens per second per GPU — a 10x improvement over v0.2. The architecture was scaling.

v0.4 (December 2025) — Latency Breakthrough

v0.4 was a latency-focused release. The headline was a 40% reduction in per-output-token latency for DeepSeek V3.1 on H200 hardware. This came from a combination of optimizations: better scheduling to reduce queue wait time, more efficient KV cache transfer, and improved decode-phase batching.

Speculative decoding shipped in this release. A smaller draft model generates candidate tokens, and the main model verifies them in parallel. When the draft model's predictions are correct (which happens frequently for predictable text), multiple tokens can be confirmed in a single forward pass. This is a well-known technique, but integrating it into the distributed routing framework — where the draft model and main model might be on different pods — required careful engineering.

CPU memory tiering for prefix cache offload addressed the problem of GPU memory exhaustion. When a pod's GPU memory filled up with cached prefixes, the least-recently-used cache entries could now be offloaded to CPU memory rather than evicted entirely. This preserved cache entries that might be needed again soon, at the cost of slightly higher retrieval latency. For workloads with many distinct prefixes, this significantly improved cache hit rates. TPU and Intel XPU disaggregation support also expanded in this release.

v0.5 "Sustaining Performance at Scale" (February 2026) — The Scale Release

v0.5 was the release that answered the question: "Does this architecture actually work at scale?" The answer was emphatic.

Hierarchical KV offloading extended the v0.4 CPU tiering into a full three-tier hierarchy: GPU memory (hot), CPU memory (warm), and disk (cold). This prevented the "cache eviction storms" that plagued large deployments — when GPU memory filled up and multiple cache entries were evicted simultaneously, causing a cascade of cache misses and prefill recomputations. With three tiers, the system could gracefully degrade rather than cliff-edge.

Cache-aware LoRA routing solved the multi-adapter problem. The EPP now tracked which LoRA adapters were loaded on which pods and routed requests to pods that had the correct adapter already in memory. Loading a LoRA adapter is fast (seconds), but at scale, the difference between routing to a pod with the adapter loaded versus one that needs to load it was the difference between meeting and missing latency SLAs.

Active-active high availability eliminated single points of failure. Multiple EPP instances could run simultaneously, sharing state through a lightweight coordination protocol. If one EPP went down, the others continued routing without interruption.

Scale-to-zero autoscaling cut costs for bursty workloads. Pods for infrequently-used models could scale to zero replicas and spin up on demand when requests arrived. This was particularly valuable for enterprises serving many models with uneven traffic patterns.

The benchmark numbers told the story. On a 16x16 B200 topology (16 prefill GPUs, 16 decode GPUs), llm-d sustained approximately 50,000 output tokens per second, or roughly 3,100 tokens per second per decode GPU. From 45 tok/s in v0.1 to 50,000 tok/s in v0.5 — a 1,000x improvement in nine months.

v0.7 (June 2026) — Maturity

After the performance fireworks of v0.5, v0.7 was deliberately quieter. The focus shifted from peak performance to operational maturity — the kind of work that doesn't make for exciting benchmarks but makes the difference between a project that experts can run and a project that teams can adopt.

CUDA 13.0.2 upgrade brought the latest NVIDIA toolkit support. Standalone mode became the default deployment pattern, eliminating the dependency on external orchestrator components for simple deployments. A new user could now go from "git clone" to "running inference" with fewer moving parts. The documentation overhaul treated docs as a first-class feature, not an afterthought. CI coverage expansion closed gaps in automated testing. The project was optimizing for the new user's first hour, not just the expert's peak throughput — a sign that llm-d was transitioning from "impressive technology" to "reliable infrastructure."

The roughly 1,000x improvement from v0.1 to v0.5 did not come from a single breakthrough. It came from three compounding sources.

Better routing (cache hits). Prefix-cache-aware scheduling meant that the system did less redundant work. Every cache hit saved a full prefill computation. At 80%+ hit rates, this alone delivered a 3-5x effective throughput improvement on prefill-heavy workloads.

Disaggregation (hardware-optimal workloads). Running prefill on compute-optimized pods and decode on memory-optimized pods meant each GPU spent more of its time doing useful work. Prefill pods could saturate their tensor cores. Decode pods could saturate their memory bandwidth. Neither sat idle waiting for the other phase to finish.

Scale-out (more GPUs with efficient utilization). Wide expert parallelism across nodes, combined with NIXL's fast KV cache transfer, meant that adding more GPUs actually delivered near-linear throughput scaling rather than diminishing returns. The 16x16 B200 topology in v0.5 achieved roughly 3,100 tok/s per decode GPU, meaning that utilization efficiency remained high even at 32-GPU scale.

The combination of these three factors is what makes distributed inference optimization qualitatively different from single-node optimization. Single-node work has a ceiling defined by the hardware. Distributed optimization has a much higher ceiling, but only if the coordination overhead is low enough that adding nodes actually helps. llm-d's architecture — lightweight EPP routing, efficient cache transfer, hardware-aware scheduling — kept that overhead low.

Competitive Landscape

llm-d does not exist in a vacuum. The distributed inference space has attracted serious engineering effort from multiple directions, and understanding where llm-d fits requires understanding what the alternatives do and don't do.

NVIDIA Dynamo

Dynamo is NVIDIA's own distributed inference framework, and it is the most frequently compared project. Dynamo focuses on the execution and runtime layer: optimizing how inference computations are scheduled and executed across NVIDIA GPUs, with deep integration into NVIDIA's hardware stack (NVLink, NVSwitch, CUDA graphs). llm-d focuses on the routing and orchestration layer: deciding which pod handles which request, managing KV cache topology, and integrating with Kubernetes-native infrastructure.

In March 2026, NVIDIA announced a formal Dynamo integration with llm-d. This was a significant signal of ecosystem maturity — the two projects are complementary, not competitive. Dynamo optimizes execution within a node or tightly-coupled node group. llm-d optimizes placement and routing across the fleet. A production deployment can use both: Dynamo for execution efficiency, llm-d for fleet-level orchestration. The fact that NVIDIA chose to integrate rather than compete suggests that the routing/orchestration layer is genuinely a different problem from the execution/runtime layer.

TensorRT-LLM

NVIDIA's TensorRT-LLM is an inference optimization library that compiles models into highly optimized execution plans for NVIDIA GPUs. It operates at a fundamentally different level of the stack than llm-d. TensorRT-LLM optimizes how a single model runs on a single node (or tightly coupled multi-GPU node). llm-d uses vLLM as its inference engine, and vLLM can in turn use TensorRT-LLM as a backend for execution. The two are layered, not competing: TensorRT-LLM makes each individual inference faster, llm-d ensures that the right inference happens on the right hardware with the right cached state.

SGLang

SGLang is a programming framework for LLM applications that also includes a serving runtime. Its strengths are in structured generation (constraining outputs to match schemas), the programming model (composing LLM calls like function calls), and co-optimization of the programming model with the serving runtime. llm-d and SGLang solve different problems: SGLang optimizes how you write LLM applications and how individual requests are served. llm-d optimizes how a fleet of inference pods handles many concurrent requests across many models. llm-d is engine-agnostic in principle — the EPP routes to pods regardless of what inference engine runs inside them — so a future integration where SGLang serves as the engine inside llm-d-managed pods is architecturally possible.

Ray Serve

Ray Serve is a general-purpose model serving framework built on the Ray distributed computing library. Its strengths are multi-model serving, autoscaling, and the ability to compose arbitrary Python-based model pipelines. However, Ray Serve is general-purpose by design. It does not have LLM-specific optimizations like KV cache-aware routing, prefill/decode disaggregation, or LoRA adapter tracking. For LLM inference specifically, llm-d's purpose-built architecture delivers optimizations that a general-purpose framework cannot match without essentially reimplementing llm-d's routing logic. Ray Serve remains a strong choice for non-LLM model serving or for heterogeneous pipelines that mix LLMs with other model types.

The Ecosystem Is Layering, Not Consolidating

The key insight from the competitive landscape is that the inference stack is layering, not consolidating. Execution engines (TensorRT-LLM, vLLM), programming frameworks (SGLang), runtime orchestration (Dynamo), fleet routing (llm-d), and general-purpose serving (Ray Serve) are settling into distinct layers. The winners will be projects that play well with others, not projects that try to own the entire stack. llm-d's Kubernetes-native, engine-agnostic design positions it well in this layered world.

Academic Foundations

llm-d's technical innovations did not emerge from thin air. They built on a body of academic research that provided the theoretical foundations and initial implementations for the key techniques the project relies on. Understanding these foundations is essential to understanding why llm-d's architecture works.

vLLM and PagedAttention (UC Berkeley)

The breakthrough that made modern LLM serving practical was PagedAttention, introduced by Woosuk Kwon, Zhuohan Li, Sicheng Lin, and others in the UC Berkeley group led by Ion Stoica. PagedAttention treats the KV cache like virtual memory, with paging. Instead of pre-allocating contiguous memory blocks for each request's cache (which leads to severe memory fragmentation and waste), PagedAttention allocates cache memory in small, non-contiguous blocks and manages them with a page table, exactly like an operating system manages virtual memory.

This was transformative. Memory utilization went from 50-60% (due to fragmentation in contiguous allocation schemes) to 95%+ with PagedAttention. More efficient memory use meant more requests could be served concurrently, which meant higher throughput. vLLM — the inference engine built around PagedAttention — became the de facto standard for LLM serving, and it is the engine that runs inside each llm-d inference pod. The UC Berkeley team are founding partners of llm-d, ensuring that the engine and the orchestration layer evolve together.

LMCache and Prefix Caching Research (University of Chicago)

The University of Chicago's LMCache project introduced systematic prefix caching for LLM inference. The insight was deceptively simple: many LLM requests share common prefixes. Every request to a customer-service chatbot shares the same system prompt. Every request using few-shot prompting shares the same examples. Every request in a multi-turn conversation shares the entire conversation history up to that point.

LMCache showed that by caching the KV state for these shared prefixes and reusing it across requests, you could eliminate redundant computation for the shared portions. This research directly informed llm-d's cache-aware routing strategy: the EPP does not just know whether a pod has cache; it knows which specific prefixes a pod has cached and scores pods based on the match ratio between the incoming request's prefix and the pod's cached state. The University of Chicago team are founding partners of llm-d.

NIXL (NVIDIA)

NVIDIA's Interconnect Library (NIXL) provides high-performance memory transfer between GPU and CPU memory, between GPUs on different nodes, and across different interconnect fabrics (NVLink, InfiniBand, PCIe, RDMA). For llm-d, NIXL is the critical enabler of practical prefill/decode disaggregation. When a prefill pod completes processing a request and needs to transfer the KV cache to a decode pod, that transfer needs to happen at microsecond latency and multi-gigabyte-per-second bandwidth. NIXL provides both, using whatever the fastest available interconnect is. Without NIXL (or something like it), the cache transfer latency would dominate the disaggregation benefit, making the technique impractical. The NIXL integration in v0.3 was the release that made disaggregation work at production latency targets.

Governance: CNCF Sandbox

March 24, 2026 — KubeCon Europe, Amsterdam

llm-d is accepted into the CNCF Sandbox. Donated by IBM Research, Red Hat, and Google Cloud. The Technical Oversight Committee votes to accept, establishing llm-d as a vendor-neutral project with a path toward becoming a foundational piece of the cloud-native AI infrastructure stack.

The Cloud Native Computing Foundation (CNCF) Sandbox is the entry point for early-stage projects that solve real problems for the cloud-native ecosystem. It is not a rubber stamp. Projects must demonstrate multiple contributors from different organizations, genuine community momentum, and a clear fit within the cloud-native landscape. The CNCF Technical Oversight Committee (TOC) evaluates each application and votes on acceptance.

For llm-d, Sandbox acceptance meant several concrete things.

Vendor-neutral governance. No single company controls the roadmap. IBM Research, Red Hat, and Google Cloud each donated intellectual property and engineering resources, but the project's direction is now governed by the contributor community, not by any one company's product roadmap. This is not a theoretical protection — it is the structural guarantee that prevents the "rug pull" scenario where a dominant vendor steers the project to serve its own interests at the community's expense.

CNCF infrastructure. Sandbox projects receive access to CNCF's CI infrastructure, security audit programs, trademark protection, and the visibility that comes with being listed alongside Kubernetes, Prometheus, Envoy, and the rest of the CNCF landscape. For a project like llm-d that aims to be infrastructure, this credibility matters enormously for enterprise adoption.

A path forward. The CNCF graduation path goes from Sandbox to Incubating to Graduated. Incubating status requires demonstrated production adoption, robust governance documentation, and completion of a security audit. Graduated status (the level Kubernetes, Prometheus, and Envoy hold) requires broad adoption and a proven governance model. For llm-d, the Sandbox acceptance is the starting line, not the finish line. The path from Sandbox to Incubating will require proving that the project's governance scales as well as its technology.

For contributors, CNCF governance means that contributions are protected by a neutral IP policy (the Apache 2.0 license plus CNCF's CLA), the project has a clear code of conduct, and there is a transparent process for becoming a maintainer, joining governance bodies, and influencing the project's direction. This is what makes multi-company open-source collaboration sustainable over the long term.

Historically, the projects that have navigated this path most successfully — Kubernetes, Prometheus, Envoy, Containerd — share a common trait: they solved a real infrastructure problem that no single vendor could own. llm-d's bet is that distributed LLM inference routing is that kind of problem.

Forward-Looking: What v1.0 Might Look Like

Seven releases in, llm-d has proven its core thesis: cache-aware routing and prefill/decode disaggregation work, they work at scale, and they work across hardware vendors. But the problems that remain unsolved are at least as interesting as the ones that have been solved. Here is what v1.0 might need to include before the project can claim the stability and completeness that a 1.0 version number implies.

Multi-Cluster Federation

Today, llm-d routes within a single Kubernetes cluster. But enterprises increasingly run inference workloads across multiple clusters — different regions for latency, different cloud providers for redundancy, on-premise clusters for data sovereignty. Multi-cluster federation would give the EPP a fleet-wide view, routing requests to the best available pod regardless of which cluster it lives in. This requires solving cross-cluster KV cache state sharing, which is a hard distributed systems problem with significant latency implications.

Model-Aware Autoscaling

Current autoscaling treats inference pods as undifferentiated workloads. But a 7B model and a 70B model have fundamentally different scaling curves. The 7B model can serve high throughput on a single GPU and scales well by adding pods. The 70B model requires tensor parallelism across multiple GPUs per pod and has a much more complex scaling function. Model-aware autoscaling would encode these characteristics into the scaling decisions, choosing the right number and size of pods for each model based on its specific hardware requirements and observed load patterns.

Deeper Hardware Integration

The accelerator landscape is diversifying rapidly. NVIDIA B200, AMD MI300X, Intel Gaudi 3, Google TPU v6 — each has different strengths. Some models run better on hardware with high memory bandwidth. Others benefit from raw compute throughput. Hardware-aware scheduling that matches model architectures to silicon strengths would allow a heterogeneous cluster to serve each model on the hardware that suits it best, improving both performance and cost efficiency.

Agentic Inference Patterns

As AI agents become primary consumers of LLM inference, the workload patterns are changing. Agents make chains of LLM calls — tool calls, chain-of-thought reasoning, multi-turn planning — where each call depends on the previous one's output. The routing layer needs to handle these patterns differently than simple request/response. An agent's entire chain should ideally route to the same pod (or pod group) to maximize KV cache reuse across the chain. This requires the EPP to understand request chains, not just individual requests.

Cost-Aware Routing

Not every request needs the fastest hardware. A batch summarization job can tolerate higher latency in exchange for running on cheaper spot instances. Cost-aware routing would allow the EPP to factor in hardware cost alongside cache affinity, queue depth, and latency targets — routing to the cheapest available hardware that still meets the request's SLA.

The Unsolved Frontiers

Beyond v1.0, there are problems that the field has not yet figured out how to solve efficiently. Multi-modal inference routing — how do you cache and route image embeddings alongside text KV caches? Cross-cloud KV cache transfer — is it even practical to transfer gigabytes of cache state across cloud boundaries? Inference-time compute scaling — can the routing layer dynamically allocate more "thinking time" (more compute budget) to harder problems while spending less on easy ones?

What "1.0" ultimately means is: stable APIs that don't break between releases, backward compatibility guarantees that enterprises can rely on, and production hardening at a level where the open-source version is deployable without the enterprise wrapper for organizations that have the operational maturity to run it themselves.

Thirteen Months In

From a Summit stage announcement to a CNCF Sandbox project. From a prototype that routed at 45 tokens per second to a production system that sustains 50,000. From a coalition of five core partners to an ecosystem of thirteen-plus organizations spanning chip vendors, cloud providers, AI companies, and research universities. Thirteen months is not a long time, and the speed of llm-d's trajectory is unusual even by open-source infrastructure standards.

But so is the problem. Distributed LLM inference is the kind of challenge that compresses timelines because it has to. The models are getting bigger. The demand is growing exponentially. The cost of inefficient serving is measured in millions of dollars of wasted GPU-hours. Every month of delay is a month where organizations are either over-provisioning expensive hardware or under-serving their users. The urgency is real, and it shows in the release cadence.

What made this speed possible was the convergence of three factors. The right technical insight: cache-aware routing is a genuinely novel approach to a problem that traditional infrastructure tools cannot solve, and it delivered measurable, large improvements from the first release that implemented it. The right coalition: multi-vendor from day one, with academic research partners providing the theoretical foundations and industry partners providing the engineering scale and production validation. The right governance model: open-source upstream, enterprise downstream, CNCF neutral ground, with a clear commitment that the open-source project leads and the enterprise product follows.

The next year will test whether this model scales — not just technically, but organizationally. Can a CNCF Sandbox project move at the speed the AI inference market demands while maintaining the governance rigor that enterprise adopters require? Can a multi-vendor coalition sustain collaboration as the inference market becomes more competitive? Can the project absorb new contributors fast enough to avoid the burnout that kills promising open-source projects?

These are organizational questions, not technical ones. And in the long run, they matter more than any benchmark number. But thirteen months in, the trajectory is clear. llm-d has demonstrated that distributed LLM inference is an infrastructure problem, that infrastructure problems have infrastructure solutions, and that those solutions work best when they are built in the open, by a broad coalition, for the benefit of the entire ecosystem.

The foundation is laid. The architecture is proven. The ecosystem is forming. What happens next will be determined not by what the technology can do, but by how well the community can govern its growth.
