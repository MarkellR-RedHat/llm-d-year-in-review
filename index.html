The Beginning

The Problem

LLM inference was broken. Not in a subtle way, but in a fundamental, architectural way. Traditional load balancers treated every GPU pod as interchangeable, randomly spraying requests across a cluster. But LLMs are not stateless web servers. They carry enormous context in their KV caches, and the right pod, one that already holds relevant cached data, can serve a request 10x faster than a random one.

Every random routing decision was burning GPU cycles that could never be recovered. The industry was leaving performance on the table, not by percentages, but by orders of magnitude.

Organizations deploying LLMs at scale had two options: build a bespoke inference stack from scratch (expensive, fragile, single-vendor), or accept terrible utilization and unpredictable latency. Neither was acceptable. The Kubernetes ecosystem, the standard for running everything else in production, had no answer for this problem.

llm-d was built to fix that. Not with incremental patches, but with a ground-up rethinking of how inference requests should be routed, scheduled, and served across distributed GPU infrastructure. This is the story of what happened next.

The Moment That Changed Everything

llm-d was accepted into the CNCF Sandbox, the fastest path from announcement to acceptance in recent memory. Just 10 months from launch to vendor-neutral governance under the foundation that stewards Kubernetes itself.

This was more than a governance milestone. It was a signal to the industry: distributed LLM inference is too important to be owned by any single vendor. The CNCF acceptance validated the architecture, the community, and the mission, and opened the door for every organization that had been waiting for a neutral standard.
